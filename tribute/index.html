Mahatma Gandhi

"Be the change that you wish to see in the world"

Early Life

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi was born on 2 October 1869 into a Gujarati Hindu Modh Baniya family in Porbandar (also known as Sudamapuri), a coastal town on the Kathiawar Peninsula and then part of the small princely state of Porbandar in the Kathiawar Agency of the Indian Empire. His father, Karamchand Uttamchand Gandhi (1822–1885), served as the diwan (chief minister) of Porbandar state.

Although he only had an elementary education and had previously been a clerk in the state administration, Karamchand proved a capable chief minister. During his tenure, Karamchand married four times. His first two wives died young, after each had given birth to a daughter, and his third marriage was childless. In 1857, Karamchand sought his third wife's permission to remarry; that year, he married Putlibai (1844–1891), who also came from Junagadh, and was from a Pranami Vaishnava family. Karamchand and Putlibai had three children over the ensuing decade: a son, Laxmidas (c. 1860–1914); a daughter, Raliatbehn (1862–1960); and another son, Karsandas (c. 1866–1913).

A Tribute to a Legacy of Peace

In the tapestry of human history, few figures shine as brightly as Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi. More than just a leader of India's independence movement, he was a beacon of truth, a champion of nonviolence, and a profound moral compass for the world. Born in a time of colonial oppression, Gandhi dared to dream of a free India, not through the clash of arms, but through the strength of satyagraha – the power of truth and nonviolent resistance. He showed us that even the mightiest empires could be challenged by the quiet determination of a people united in their pursuit of justice. His life was his message. He lived simply, embracing humility and self-reliance. He walked thousands of miles, not for conquest, but for connection, bridging divides and inspiring millions. He fasted not for personal gain, but to awaken the conscience of a nation and to challenge the very foundations of injustice. Gandhi's philosophy extended far beyond political liberation. He emphasized the importance of self-rule, not just for nations, but for individuals. He advocated for the upliftment of the marginalized, promoting equality and social justice for all. He saw the interconnectedness of all beings, advocating for a world free from violence and exploitation. His legacy endures, not just in the independent India he helped create, but in the countless movements for peace and justice that have drawn inspiration from his example. From Martin Luther King Jr.'s civil rights struggle to Nelson Mandela's fight against apartheid, Gandhi's teachings have resonated across continents and generations. Today, as we face challenges of conflict, inequality, and environmental degradation, Gandhi's message remains as relevant as ever. He reminds us that true strength lies not in force, but in compassion. He teaches us that change is possible, even in the face of seemingly insurmountable odds. He encourages us to be the change we wish to see in the world. Let us honor his memory by embracing his principles of truth, nonviolence, and selfless service. Let us strive to build a world where justice prevails, where peace reigns, and where the spirit of Mahatma Gandhi continues to guide us towards a brighter future.
